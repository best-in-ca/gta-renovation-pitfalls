Renovation Contract Essentials

What every renovation contract must include before you sign. A short, fixable list — but ignored at your peril.

A renovation contract isn't a legal document until something goes wrong. Then it's the single most important paper you'll have in the dispute. Most homeowners sign the contractor's standard contract without reading it carefully. The fixes below take 30 minutes to add and save thousands when things drift sideways.

The scope section

Detailed line-item scope

Not a paragraph saying "kitchen renovation." A list of every space affected, every surface treated, every fixture replaced. Specifically:

  • Demolition: what comes out, who hauls it
  • Rough work: framing, plumbing, electrical, HVAC by line
  • Drywall and insulation scope
  • Cabinet specification: brand, line, model number
  • Counter specification: material, edge, thickness, sample
  • Tile specification: brand, SKU, pattern
  • Plumbing fixtures: brand, model
  • Lighting: every fixture listed
  • Flooring: material, brand, finish
  • Paint: rooms, brand, sheen

The benchmark: someone unfamiliar with the project could buy all the materials from the spec alone.

Material allowances (clearly marked)

Where homeowner selects later, the allowance is the line-item dollar amount. The contract should specify:

  • Each allowance with category, amount, and what grade it represents
  • How over/under reconciles (most reasonable: actual cost +/- difference)
  • Markup on materials specified by homeowner (industry standard 15–25%)

The financial section

Payment schedule

Standard structure:

  • 20–30% deposit at signing
  • Milestone payments at framing, rough-in, drywall, finishes (e.g., 4 payments of 15% each)
  • Final 10–15% on completion (held until punch list closes)

Watch for: deposits over 40%, full payment before completion, cash-only requests.

Change order process

Every scope change should require a written change order with:

  • Description of the change
  • Cost impact
  • Timeline impact
  • Both parties' signatures

Verbal "yes, just do it" changes cause most disputes. Insist on the paper trail.

Contingency disclosure

How the contractor handles surprises behind walls. Should specify either:

  • A contingency line in the contract (10–15% typical) that's only billed if used, OR
  • A clear process for handling unforeseen conditions discovered during demo

The timeline section

Start date and target completion

Both should be specific dates, not "approximately." Reputable contractors like Red Stone Contracting commit to these dates and adjust them only with notice.

Milestone schedule

For major projects, milestone dates for: demo complete, rough-in complete, drywall complete, finishes complete. Helps both sides track progress.

Delay provisions

What happens if the contractor is late through their fault? Most contracts are silent here. Reasonable to negotiate a small daily delay credit ($100–250) for contractor-caused delays beyond the target completion date.

The legal protections section

Insurance certificates

Contractor must provide:

  • General liability insurance (minimum $2M coverage)
  • WSIB clearance certificate (current)
  • Certificates of insurance for any major subcontractors

All three should be appended to or referenced in the contract.

Warranty terms

The contract should specify warranty length and coverage for:

  • Workmanship (typically 2–3 years for general contracting)
  • Materials (manufacturer warranties pass through)
  • What's excluded (homeowner-caused damage, normal wear, climate extremes)

Lien protection

The Ontario Construction Act protects homeowners from contractor liens for unpaid subs. The contract should explicitly require:

  • Contractor pays all subcontractors before requesting final payment
  • Contractor provides statutory declaration of payment at project end
  • Liens from subs are contractor's responsibility, not homeowner's

The dispute resolution section

How disagreements get handled

Most contracts default to litigation, which is slow and expensive. Reasonable options to add:

  • Mediation as first step (cost-shared between parties)
  • Specific deadlines for raising concerns
  • Contact escalation path (who to contact when standard communication breaks down)

Termination clauses

How the contract can be ended by either party:

  • For cause (contractor abandonment, missed deadlines, bad workmanship)
  • For convenience (homeowner cancellation — usually with a kill fee covering work done plus a percentage)

The contract red flags

  • Lump-sum quote without detail — unenforceable when issues arise
  • "Time is of the essence" without specific dates — meaningless
  • "All applicable permits included" without specifying who pulls them
  • Mandatory binding arbitration in favor of contractor — fairness concern
  • "Contractor not responsible for delays caused by..." with vague conditions
  • Waiver of homeowner inspection rights
  • Authority to substitute "equivalent or better" materials without notice
  • Liens-allowed clauses

Before you sign

  • Read every page — including the standard terms in fine print
  • Cross out clauses you don't agree to (and have both parties initial)
  • Add missing protections in writing
  • Consider lawyer review for projects over $100K (typical lawyer review: 1–2 hours, $300–700)
  • Keep an original signed copy in a safe place
  • Photocopy and store in cloud backup
