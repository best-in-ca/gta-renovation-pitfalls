Renovation Material Selection

Cabinets, counters, tile, fixtures, flooring — what's worth paying for, where to save, and how to evaluate quality.

Material selection is where most renovation budgets blow up. The labour cost is roughly fixed; materials can vary 3–5x for similar-looking products. This guide walks through each major category — what quality differences actually matter, where premium pays off, and where you're just paying for branding.

Cabinets

The single largest line item in most kitchens. Three quality tiers:

Stock cabinets ($200–350/linear foot)

  • Pre-sized boxes from major suppliers (IKEA, Home Depot, big-box)
  • Particle board boxes, basic hardware
  • Limited finish options
  • Lead time 2–6 weeks

Right for: rentals, secondary kitchens, budget-constrained projects in homes priced under $1M.

Semi-custom ($400–700/linear foot)

  • Plywood box upgrade option
  • Wider door style and finish selection
  • Some custom sizing
  • Better hardware (soft-close standard)
  • Lead time 6–10 weeks

Right for: most Toronto-area renovations. The sweet spot for cost and quality.

Custom cabinetry ($700–1,400/linear foot)

  • Built to exact specification from millwork shop
  • Plywood or solid wood construction
  • Any door style, any finish
  • Premium hardware
  • Lead time 10–16 weeks

Right for: homes priced over $1.5M, unusual layouts, designer-led kitchens. See kitchen renovation services for typical custom scope.

Countertops

Laminate ($25–45/sq ft installed)

Modern laminate (Formica, Wilsonart) is dramatically better than 1990s laminate. Looks like stone in photos. Good for rentals, secondary kitchens, basement bars.

Engineered stone / quartz ($60–120/sq ft installed)

The current default. Cambria, Caesarstone, Silestone are the major brands. Non-porous, no sealing, consistent pattern. Works for 90% of Toronto kitchens.

Natural stone — granite, marble, soapstone ($80–250/sq ft installed)

  • Granite: Durable, varied pattern, requires sealing every 1–2 years
  • Marble: Beautiful, soft, stains and etches. High-maintenance choice
  • Soapstone: Naturally non-porous, develops patina, expensive

Premium engineered ($150–300/sq ft installed)

Dekton, Neolith — ultra-compact surfaces. Heat and UV resistant, suitable for outdoor kitchens. Premium price.

Tile

Porcelain (most common, $4–15/sq ft material)

Hard-bodied, low-absorption tile. The default for floors and bathrooms. Wide quality range — cheap porcelain looks cheap, mid-range looks excellent.

Ceramic ($3–10/sq ft material)

Lower-strength than porcelain. Fine for walls and backsplashes, less ideal for floors in high-traffic areas.

Natural stone — marble, travertine, slate ($12–30/sq ft material)

Beautiful, requires sealing, higher installation cost. Used in master bathrooms and feature spaces. See bathroom renovation services.

Large-format tile ($8–25/sq ft material)

Tiles 24×24 inches or larger create a seamless look. Installation requires very flat substrate and skilled installer. Adds $4–8/sq ft to install labour.

Installation labour

Standard tile install runs $8–14/sq ft labour. Large-format, mosaic, or special patterns push to $15–25/sq ft. Backsplash work runs higher per sq ft than floor work because of cuts.

Plumbing fixtures

Builder grade ($150–400 per fixture)

Functional, basic finish quality, often nickel or chrome only. Standard at the lowest-end of new homes. Replaceable but not aspirational.

Mid-range ($300–800 per fixture)

Brands like Moen, Delta (mid-line), Riobel. Reasonable finish quality, better cartridges, longer warranties. The sweet spot.

Premium ($600–2,500 per fixture)

Kohler, Hansgrohe, Brizo, Grohe. Designer finishes, advanced features, longer warranties. Worth it in master bathrooms, kitchens you'll use daily.

Luxury ($1,500–8,000+ per fixture)

Waterworks, Newport Brass, Aquabrass premium lines. Architectural quality, lifetime warranties on some lines. Specifier-driven, often selected by designers.

Lighting

One of the most-overlooked budget categories. The numbers:

  • Big-box recessed cans: $40–80 each
  • Quality LED recessed: $100–250 each
  • Chandeliers/pendants: $200–3,000+ each
  • Under-cabinet lighting: $400–1,200 per kitchen
  • Smart lighting controls: $200–800 additional

A typical kitchen renovation includes 8–14 light fixtures. Budget $1,500–6,000 for the lighting package.

Flooring

Engineered hardwood ($7–14/sq ft installed)

The default for most Toronto homes. Real wood veneer over a plywood core. Stable in humidity changes, can be refinished 1–2 times.

Solid hardwood ($9–18/sq ft installed)

Traditional, refinishable multiple times. Sensitive to humidity changes. Best for above-grade installations.

Luxury vinyl plank ($4–9/sq ft installed)

Modern LVP is genuinely good — looks like wood, water-resistant, durable. Excellent for basements and kitchens.

Tile

Covered in tile section above. Best for wet areas (bathrooms, mudrooms, basements).

Carpet ($3–10/sq ft installed)

Lost popularity but still appropriate in bedrooms and dedicated home theatres.

Hardware (the small-budget category that matters)

Cabinet pulls and knobs, door handles, faucet finishes — small items, big visual impact. Mid-range hardware ($8–25 per piece) is the sweet spot. Cheap hardware ($2–5) feels cheap; designer hardware ($40–150) doesn't deliver proportional value.
