Working With Trades During a Renovation

Plumbers, electricians, HVAC techs, drywallers, tilers — how to manage the trades as a homeowner, when the general contractor handles it, and what's reasonable to ask.

A renovation isn't one contractor's work — it's a parade of trades, each in for 1–5 days. Knowing how to coordinate, what's reasonable to expect, and where the GC's responsibility ends and yours begins makes the project smoother for everyone.

Who does what — the typical trade lineup

The general contractor

Coordinates everything. Pulls permits, schedules trades, manages communication, owns the final result. The GC is your single point of contact. For a project with Red Stone Contracting, the GC handles all trade coordination as part of standard scope.

Demolition crew

Often part of the GC's regular team or contracted out. 1–3 days at the start of most projects.

Framer / carpenter

Structural work, new walls, openings. Usually the GC's in-house team.

Plumber (licensed)

Drains, water lines, fixture rough-in. Must be Ontario-licensed. Visits twice on most projects — rough-in plus trim-out.

Electrician (licensed)

New circuits, panel changes, lighting rough-in and trim. ESA permit required for permitted electrical work.

HVAC technician

If ductwork or mechanical changes. Coordinated with electrical for new circuits.

Drywall crew

Hanging, taping, mudding, sanding. 3–6 day phase usually.

Insulator

Before drywall. Often same day or short stretch.

Painter

After drywall, sometimes after some finishes. See painting services for typical scope when coordinated with renovation projects.

Tile setter

For bathrooms, backsplashes, floors. Skill level varies enormously — a great tile setter makes the bathroom; a mediocre one makes it look DIY.

Flooring installer

Often the last trade in (after drywall and paint). Sometimes earlier for engineered hardwood.

Cabinet installer

From the cabinet supplier or GC's team. Critical that this happens before counter templating.

Counter fabricator

Templates after cabinets, fabricates off-site, returns to install 1–2 weeks later.

Finish carpenter

Trim, baseboards, crown moulding. Final-week work.

What the GC handles vs what you handle

GC handles

  • Trade scheduling and coordination
  • Permits and inspections
  • Trade communications about scope
  • Resolving issues between trades
  • Material delivery coordination
  • Quality control across trades
  • Payment to trades and lien waivers

Homeowner handles

  • Material selections (cabinets, tile, fixtures, lighting, paint, flooring)
  • Access to the home for trades
  • Decisions when surprises require homeowner input
  • Communication with GC (not directly with trades, generally)
  • Punch list review at end
  • Final payment release

Communication norms

Talk to the GC, not directly to trades

When a tradesperson is in your home, conversation about scope, change requests, or concerns should go through the GC. Direct homeowner-to-tradesperson scope conversations cause confusion and missed change orders.

One source of truth for decisions

If the painter asks "which white?" and you say "Decorator's White," that decision needs to flow back to the GC's records. Otherwise you risk inconsistency when other trades reference the wrong colour.

Daily check-ins are fine; constant interference isn't

End-of-day walkthroughs with the crew lead are productive. Hovering over the tradesperson during work isn't. Trust the process, raise issues with the GC.

Write down what you're told

"The plumber said it would cost $400 to add the prep sink hookup" — write this down with date. If it appears on the invoice for $800, you have a reference point.

When tradespeople are in your home

Access and parking

  • Plan parking arrangements for crews (most need close access for tool transport)
  • Provide a key plan if you'll be at work when they arrive
  • Confirm hours — trades typically work 7:30 AM to 4:30 PM in Toronto residential

Bathroom and water access

  • Designate one bathroom for crew use (often basement if available)
  • Confirm water access for cleaning tools
  • Set ground rules for kitchen use — usually limited to refilling water bottles

Pets and kids

  • Pets in another part of the house, ideally secured behind closed doors
  • Kids supervised away from work zones — open paint, sharp tools, hot pipes
  • Schedule loud work (sanding, drilling) for school hours if possible

Property protection

  • GC provides drop cloths and plastic sheeting; you confirm what's covered
  • Move fragile items, art, electronics
  • Document existing condition with photos before demo (insurance against accidental damage claims)

Expected work quality from each trade

Licensed trades — non-negotiable

Plumbing and electrical must be done by Ontario-licensed trades. The GC verifies licensing; you can ask for confirmation. ESA work requires a permit and inspection.

Skilled trades — variable quality

Tile, painting, hardwood installation, finish carpentry — quality varies dramatically with the individual installer. The GC's reputation tells you what level to expect. References from recent similar projects are worth checking.

What good work looks like

  • Even tile spacing, flat grout lines, no lippage
  • Cut-in paint lines that are sharp at every wall/ceiling/trim transition
  • Drywall joints invisible when light hits them at an angle
  • Trim that meets at clean mitered corners
  • Cabinet doors aligned and gaps consistent
  • Plumbing fixtures level and tightly fitted

Handling problems mid-project

Quality issues

Raise immediately with the GC. Document with photos. Most reputable contractors fix issues without dispute when raised promptly.

Scope or change disagreements

Reference the contract. If the work isn't in scope, it requires a change order with cost and timeline impact in writing.

Tradesperson behavior issues

Raise with the GC, not the tradesperson directly. The GC can swap personnel without making the situation awkward.

Damage to your property

Document with photos. Notify the GC immediately. Contractor's general liability insurance covers this; legitimate claims get resolved within days.
